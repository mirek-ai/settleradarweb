## Visas and Pathways to Permanent Residence

Canada does not operate a conventional work visa system for skilled workers. The standard route is a **two-step process: obtain a temporary work permit first, then pursue permanent residence through Express Entry or a Provincial Nominee Program (PNP)**.

### Step 1: Temporary Work Permit

The most common work permit for skilled expats entering Canada is the **employer-specific open work permit** or **LMIA-exempt work permit** obtained through IRCC (Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada).

**LMIA-based permits (Labour Market Impact Assessment):**
- The employer must obtain a positive LMIA from Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) proving no qualified Canadian was available for the role
- Processing: 2â€“5 months for the LMIA, then weeks for the permit
- Typically used for roles outside international trade agreements or intra-company transfers

**LMIA-exempt permits (most common for skilled workers):**
- **Intra-Company Transfer (ICT):** Managers, executives, or specialized knowledge workers transferred within a multinational company; no LMIA required; generally easier to obtain
- **International agreements (CUSMA/USMCA):** For citizens of the U.S. and Mexico in listed professional occupations; immediate entry at the border or port of entry for U.S. citizens
- **International Mobility Program (IMP):** Broad category covering significant social, cultural, or economic benefit roles; post-graduate work permits; spousal/common-law partner permits
- **Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP):** For graduates of eligible Canadian institutions; permits up to 3 years; one of the most efficient feeders into the Express Entry pool

**Work permits are typically tied to a specific employer** (employer-specific) unless otherwise designated. Open work permits â€” allowing work for any employer â€” are available for specific categories including spouses of skilled workers, international graduates (PGWP), and some IMP categories.

**Applying through IRCC's online portal: ircc.canada.ca**. Application fee: CAD $155 for a work permit (CAD $100 in some streams). Biometrics: CAD $85 per person. Processing times: 4â€“16 weeks depending on stream and country of origin.

### The 2026 Express Entry System: What Has Changed

Express Entry is Canada's points-based system for managing applications for **permanent residence** from skilled workers. It is not a work permit â€” it leads directly to PR.[^3][^1]

**How it works:**
1. Create an Express Entry profile with IRCC at **ircc.canada.ca**
2. Receive a **Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS)** score based on age, education, language, work experience, Canadian experience, and other factors
3. IRCC holds invitation rounds ("draws") â€” the highest-scoring candidates receive an Invitation to Apply (ITA)
4. Receive an ITA â†’ submit a complete PR application within 60 days
5. Application processing: approximately **6 months** for Express Entry streams

**The three federal programs (currently in effect, under reform consultation):**[^12][^1]
- **Canadian Experience Class (CEC):** For candidates with at least 1 year of skilled Canadian work experience in the past 3 years; most active draw type in 2026
- **Federal Skilled Worker Program (FSW):** For candidates with 1 year of skilled foreign work experience who meet minimum language and education requirements
- **Federal Skilled Trades Program (FSTP):** For candidates with 2 years of experience in skilled trades; job offer or Certificate of Qualification required

## Permanent Residence to Citizenship: The Straightforward Timeline

### Permanent Residence (PR)

Permanent residence is what you get at the end of Express Entry or PNP processes â€” before citizenship. A PR card confirms your status. PR holders:
- Can live, work, and study anywhere in Canada
- Have access to provincial health insurance from day one
- Must not spend more than **1,095 days outside Canada in any 5-year period** (or risk losing PR status)

**Maintaining PR:** If you spend extended time outside Canada â€” for work, family, or other reasons â€” you must be employed by a Canadian employer abroad, accompanying a Canadian citizen spouse, or employed with the Canadian government to count that time toward your residency obligation. This catches many expats off guard during international assignments.

### Canadian Citizenship

Once you have PR, citizenship requires:[^20][^21]

- **Physical presence: 1,095 days in Canada** within the 5-year period immediately before applying (3 out of 5 years)
- Days before you became a PR (as a temporary resident) count at half value â€” maximum 365 days credit
- **Income tax compliance:** Must have filed taxes for at least 3 of the 5 years in the eligibility window
- **Language proficiency:** Adequate knowledge of English or French â€” typically demonstrated by CLB 4 in speaking and listening; tested through an interview or an approved language test
- **Knowledge of Canada:** Pass the **citizenship test** â€” a 20-question multiple choice test on Canadian history, geography, government, rights and responsibilities, and symbols; passing score: 15/20; study guide: Discover Canada (freely available at canada.ca)
- **Clean record:** No criminal convictions during the eligibility period; no charges pending; not under removal order

**Dual citizenship: Fully permitted.** Canada does not require renunciation of your original nationality. A Canadian passport provides visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to approximately 185 countries.[^20]

**Application fee: CAD $630 per adult, CAD $100 per minor.**

**Processing times:** Currently **12â€“24 months** from submission. IRCC publishes updated processing times weekly at ircc.canada.ca.

### Key Deductions and Contributions

**Canada Pension Plan (CPP):**[^26]
- Employee rate: **5.95% of earnings** up to CAD $74,600 (primary earnings ceiling 2026)
- Maximum employee contribution: approximately CAD **$4,230**
- CPP2 (enhancement): additional 4% on earnings from CAD $74,600 to CAD $85,000
- Self-employed: **11.9% of earnings** (both employee and employer shares)

**Employment Insurance (EI):**
- Employee premium: **1.64% of insurable earnings** up to a ceiling (approximately CAD $1,049 maximum annual premium in 2026)
- EI entitles workers to income replacement if they lose their job (55% of insurable earnings for up to 45 weeks, depending on regional unemployment rate and work history)

**TFSA and RRSP (tax-sheltered savings vehicles):**[^26]
- **TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account):** Contribute up to **CAD $7,000/year** in 2026; investment growth and withdrawals tax-free; accumulates from age 18 (if resident in Canada)
- **RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan):** Contribute up to **CAD $33,810** in 2026 or 18% of prior year's earned income (whichever is less); contributions reduce taxable income in the year of contribution; tax deferred until withdrawal

**No wealth tax, no inheritance tax, no gift tax in Canada.** Capital gains are included in taxable income at **50% inclusion rate** (50% of the gain is taxable at your marginal rate), not a flat capital gains tax.

### GST/HST (Goods and Services / Harmonised Sales Tax)

Federal GST: **5%**. Provinces that have harmonized their sales tax with GST charge HST instead:
- Ontario: **13% HST**
- BC: **5% GST + 7% PST** (not harmonized â€” you pay both)
- Alberta: **5% GST only** â€” no provincial sales tax; this is a meaningful daily cost advantage
- Quebec: **5% GST + 9.975% QST** (Quebec Sales Tax â€” similar to HST at ~15%)
- Manitoba, Saskatchewan: 5% GST + provincial PST

Unlike Europe, prices in Canada are **not displayed tax-inclusive** â€” what you see on the shelf is before tax. The final till price is higher. This catches nearly every new expat off guard.

## Healthcare: Universal from Day One of PR, Three-Month Wait in Some Provinces

Canada's healthcare system â€” **Medicare** â€” is publicly funded, universally available to citizens and permanent residents, and free at the point of use for medically necessary physician and hospital services. The system is administered by each of the 13 provinces and territories, which means quality, coverage details, and wait times vary.[^30][^31]

### Access for Expats

**Permanent residents** are eligible for provincial healthcare from day one of landing â€” in most provinces. The key nuance: **some provinces impose a 3-month waiting period** before provincial health insurance kicks in.[^32][^33]

| Province | Waiting Period |
|----------|---------------|
| Ontario (OHIP) | **3 months** |
| British Columbia (MSP) | **3 months** |
| Alberta (AHCIP) | **No waiting period â€” coverage from date of PR** |
| Quebec (RAMQ) | **3 months** |
| Manitoba, New Brunswick, PEI | 3 months |
| Nova Scotia, Saskatchewan, Newfoundland | No waiting period or immediate coverage |

**The recommendation for Ontario, BC, and Quebec arrivals: buy private international health insurance for the first 3 months.** A gap in coverage during this period means full out-of-pocket costs for any medical care.

**Work permit holders:** Are eligible for provincial health insurance in most provinces while employed on a valid work permit of 6 months or more. Rules vary by province. Ontario OHIP: work permit of 6+ months and residing in Ontario qualifies; the 3-month waiting period still applies.

### What Medicare Covers and What It Doesn't

**Covered (free at point of use):**
- GP and family doctor visits
- Specialist referrals
- Hospital inpatient and outpatient care
- Diagnostic testing (X-ray, MRI, blood work) when medically required
- Emergency care

**Not covered (you pay out of pocket or through private insurance):**
- Prescription drugs (except in hospital â€” provincial drug plans cover some groups)
- Dental care
- Vision care
- Ambulance fees (varies by province; typically CAD $45â€“240)
- Semi-private or private hospital room upgrades
- Physiotherapy, chiropractic, psychology (limited public coverage)

Approximately **two-thirds of Canadians** carry supplemental private insurance â€” typically through an employer benefits package â€” to cover dental, prescriptions, and vision. For most expats employed full-time, your employer's group benefits plan is the first place to look. Without employer benefits, a private plan costs approximately CAD $80â€“200/month for a single adult depending on coverage.[^34][^30]

**The GP shortage:** Finding a family doctor in urban Canada â€” particularly in Toronto, Vancouver, and the Greater Toronto Area â€” is a genuine challenge. Approximately 22% of Canadians don't have a regular family doctor, and the shortage is worst in Ontario and Quebec. Walk-in clinics and virtual care platforms (Maple, Telus Health, Cleveland Clinic Canada) fill the gap for most expats in the short term.

**Emergency: 911.** Non-emergency health information: **811** (Telehealth Ontario) or provincial equivalents â€” phone triage staffed by registered nurses.

## Buying Property: Foreign Buyer Ban Until January 2027

Canada's foreign buyer situation is the most significant property restriction for expats.

**The Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act** is in force until **January 1, 2027**. This means that in 2026, if you are not a Canadian citizen or permanent resident, you are **prohibited** from purchasing most residential properties within Census Metropolitan Areas (CMAs) and Census Agglomerations (CAs).[^38][^39]

**Who is prohibited:**[^38]
- Foreign nationals who are neither citizens nor permanent residents
- Non-Canadian corporations
- Any corporate entity controlled by a foreign national

**Exceptions within the ban:**[^38]
- **Work permit holders** with at least 183 days remaining on their permit, who file Canadian income taxes, and have not purchased more than one property â€” can buy residential property (this is a significant and often-overlooked exemption)
- **Permanent residents** â€” can buy with no restrictions
- **Joint purchase with an eligible Canadian** â€” permitted
- **Properties outside CMAs/CAs** (rural and small-town properties) â€” not covered by the ban
- **Commercial properties** â€” not covered
- **New development units** â€” properties purchased directly from a developer for the purpose of development (condos pre-construction) â€” partially exempt in some interpretations

**Post-2027:** The federal government is actively reviewing whether to let the ban expire or reshape it in January 2027, examining models from Australia. Expect changes after January 1, 2027.[^40]

## Your First 30 Days: The Checklist

1. **Apply for your Social Insurance Number (SIN)** â€” visit a Service Canada office with your PR confirmation letter (COPR) and passport; SIN is a 9-digit number required before any employer can pay you, before you can open most registered accounts, and before CRA can process your taxes; processing: **immediate in person**; SIN cards are no longer issued â€” memorize the number; apply at **servicecanada.gc.ca**

2. **Set up a Canadian bank account** â€” major banks: RBC, TD, BMO, Scotiabank, CIBC; credit unions (Desjardins in Quebec, Meridian in Ontario) are solid alternatives; most accounts open same-day with PR/COPR confirmation and passport; key products: chequing account (for salary and bill payments), savings account, TFSA (open immediately â€” 2026 contribution room: CAD $7,000); Wise and Revolut also work as transitional accounts but lack TFSA/RRSP access[^26]

3. **Register for provincial health insurance immediately** â€” apply the day you land; in Ontario, submit your OHIP application online at **ontario.ca/page/applying-ohip-and-getting-health-card**; even if a 3-month waiting period applies, the coverage start date is tied to your application date and arrival date â€” a delayed application delays coverage; bring your COPR, passport, and proof of Ontario address

4. **Get private travel/health insurance for the first 3 months if in Ontario, BC, or Quebec** â€” the 3-month provincial waiting period is a real gap; travel insurance with medical coverage for the period before OHIP/MSP/RAMQ kicks in costs approximately CAD $50â€“150/month; providers: Manulife CoverMe, Blue Cross, Sun Life

5. **Apply for your PR card** â€” your COPR (Confirmation of Permanent Residence) is not a PR card; apply for the physical PR card at **canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship**; processing: 8â€“12 weeks; needed for re-entry to Canada after travel abroad; submit this application as soon as possible after landing

6. **Register with the CRA (Canada Revenue Agency) and set up My Account** â€” at **canada.ca/en/revenue-agency**; CRA My Account is your portal for tax filing, checking benefit eligibility, viewing TFSA room, and managing RRSP contributions; your SIN is required; set up login with CRA-verified credentials or sign-in partner (major bank login); also register your address with CRA â€” benefits including the GST/HST Credit and Canada Child Benefit (if applicable) are sent to your CRA-registered address

7. **Get a Canadian credit card as soon as possible** â€” building Canadian credit history starts from zero when you land; even with excellent foreign credit history, you will likely start with a secured card (requires a deposit) or a starter card with a low limit; Scotiabank Scene+ and TD Cash Back have newcomer programs with reduced credit history requirements; the earlier you start, the sooner you have the credit score needed for a mortgage or apartment lease

8. **Apply for an Ontario (or provincial) driver's licence if you plan to drive** â€” book a G1 knowledge test at **drivetest.ca**; if your home country has a reciprocal agreement with your province (many do â€” check the provincial ministry of transport), you may be able to exchange your foreign licence directly; otherwise, full G1-G2-G road test sequence required, which takes approximately 20 months (G2 after 12 months of G1, G after 8 further months minimum)

9. **Understand your CRS score and Express Entry timeline if still pursuing PR** â€” if you landed on a work permit and are building Canadian experience: every additional year of Canadian work experience adds CRS points; IELTS or TEF scores can add up to 160 CRS points; completing a one-year Canadian college certificate adds 30 points; spousal CRS contributions apply; calculate your score at **cic.gc.ca/express-entry** and track the tie-breaker dates at **canadavisa.com/express-entry-invitations-to-apply**

10. **File your first Canadian tax return by April 30** â€” in your first year, you are a **deemed Canadian tax resident** from your date of landing; all worldwide income from landing date is Canadian-taxable; report foreign accounts and assets over CAD $100,000 using **Form T1135** (Foreign Income Verification Statement); missing this form carries significant penalties; retain foreign tax records for your first return; if your employer withholds at source (PAYE-equivalent), you still file a return to access benefit credits (GST/HST Credit, possible refund of over-withheld tax); deadline: April 30 of the following year; self-employed: June 15 filing, but tax owing still due April 30
