# Moving to the USA in 2026: A No-Nonsense Digital Nomad Guide

The United States boasts a $32.4 trillion economy – far bigger than any other. But it has **no special digital-nomad visa**. Remote workers rely on standard visas: tourists (B-2) or business visas (B-1), Visa Waiver stays, or formal work permits. Each has limits and rules.

## Visas & Residency
No dedicated “nomad visa” exists. Most nomads enter on **Visa Waiver (ESTA)** status (90-day max for eligible countries). Otherwise the **B-2 tourist visa** is the easiest (no income requirement). B-2 holds can last up to **6 months per entry**. Crucially, you **cannot work for a U.S. employer** on B-2. 

To work legally, you need an employment or investment visa. Options include the H‑1B (specialized jobs, annual cap) and L‑1 (intra-company transfer). Entrepreneurs from treaty countries can apply for an **E‑2 investor visa** – which requires a “substantial” U.S. business investment. These routes demand sponsors or capital; most nomads stick with short-term stays.

## Taxes, Banking & Insurance
If you earn U.S. income or stay long enough to be a resident, the U.S. taxes worldwide income. Federal tax brackets top out at 37%, plus Social Security/Medicare contributions and state taxes (varies by state). Nonresidents typically owe tax only on U.S.-source income. Nomads working remotely for foreign companies usually aren’t taxed by the U.S. unless they meet the IRS “substantial presence” test. Seek local tax advice for details.

Opening a bank account as a foreigner requires a U.S. address and ID. Banks generally insist on a Social Security Number (SSN) or Individual Taxpayer ID (ITIN). Without SSN, consider fintech/expat-friendly services (Wise, Revolut, etc.).  

Healthcare is expensive. Foreign visitors should **buy insurance**. On American exchanges, a basic ACA plan (Bronze) runs roughly **$300–$800 per month**, depending on state and age. Short-term travel policies can be cheaper ($19–$172/month). This is wise: a single ER visit can cost thousands, and a heart attack could easily bill ~$25,000. Don’t travel uninsured.

## Community, Coworking & Housing 
Co-working networks are huge. In 2025 U.S. coworking locations jumped **15%** (nearly 9,000 spaces by early 2026). Brands like WeWork, Impact Hub, and Industrious operate nationwide; almost every city has coworking hubs. These spaces are great for networking and reliable internet. 

Coliving is growing too. Companies like **Common, Ollie, Roam,** and **Starcity** run furnished, month-to-month apartments in many cities. These co-living flats include utilities and community events – think Airbnb meets hostel. Local meetups and Facebook/Meetup groups (e.g. “Digital Nomads NYC”) also help expats find friends and advice.

For accommodations, short-term furnished rentals (Airbnb, Vrbo, Blueground, Sonder, etc.) are common. A one-month stay will cost more per night than a year lease, so compare options. Some nomads sublet apartments or join house-share groups on Facebook to save money.
